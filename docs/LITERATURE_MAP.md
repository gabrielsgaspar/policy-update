## Core Literatures

### 1. Representation and party responsiveness

- Central papers: Page and Shapiro; Stimson, MacKuen, and Erikson; Soroka and Wlezien; Adams and coauthors; Kluver and Spoon.
- What they show: parties and policy respond to public opinion, voter priorities, and issue attention under some conditions.
- How this project differs: it observes direct supporter text on a party-owned platform rather than only polls, election returns, manifestos, or roll calls.
- Obsidian links: TBD.
- Threat level to novelty: medium.

### 2. Internet, media, and political economy

- Central papers: DellaVigna and Kaplan; Falck, Gold, and Heblich; Campante, Durante, and Sobbrio; Durante, Pinotti, and Tesei; Enikolopov, Makarin, and Petrova; Fujiwara, Muller, and Schwarz.
- What they show: media technologies and internet access can affect participation, protest, party entry, polarization, and electoral outcomes.
- How this project differs: it studies what happens after a digital political entrepreneur emerges: whether the organization learns from online voice.
- Obsidian links: TBD.
- Threat level to novelty: medium.

### 3. Online feedback and agenda setting

- Central papers: Barbera et al. on issue attention and agenda setting; Gallego, Scholl, and Le Mens on politicians learning from citizen feedback.
- What they show: online signals can be used to study who leads and follows in issue attention and whether politicians respond to citizen feedback.
- How this project differs: it shifts from politicians on general social media to a party-owned digital infrastructure during party formation and institutionalization.
- Obsidian links: TBD.
- Threat level to novelty: high.

### 4. M5S, digital parties, and platform politics

- Central papers: Bordignon and Ceccarini; Mosca, Vaccari, and Valeriani; Tronconi; Gerbaudo; Deseriis; work on Lex Eletti and M5S platform politics.
- What they show: M5S is a hybrid digital party with strong claims about participation, direct democracy, and platform-mediated politics.
- How this project differs: it aims to build a large-scale, validated, longitudinal measure of whether supporter text shaped later agenda and policy.
- Obsidian links: TBD.
- Threat level to novelty: high.

### 5. Text as data and LLM measurement

- Central papers: Grimmer and Stewart; Gentzkow, Kelly, and Taddy; Manifesto Project methods; Comparative Agendas Project methods; recent work on LLMs for political text classification and validation.
- What they show: automated text analysis can support political measurement when validation and design are explicit.
- How this project differs: it applies validated issue/proposal extraction and semantic matching to a feedback-to-policy pipeline.
- Obsidian links: TBD.
- Threat level to novelty: medium.

## Positioning Options

### Option A: Digital parties as learning organizations

- Pros: clear positive contribution if comment signals predict later party outputs after controls.
- Cons: requires strong evidence on timing, common shocks, and measurement validity.
- Required evidence: recovered comments, validated labels, lead-lag structure, proposal matches, placebos.

### Option B: Limits of platform-based participation

- Pros: important even if responsiveness is weak or selective.
- Cons: needs careful framing to avoid becoming only a data-recovery/null-results paper.
- Required evidence: credible measures of supporter voice and party outputs, plus evidence that the platform observed voice but response was limited.

### Option C: Selective responsiveness under institutionalization

- Pros: likely robust to mixed findings and closely matches `PROJECT.md`.
- Cons: more complex theory and heterogeneity design.
- Required evidence: phase-specific responsiveness, issue/proposal heterogeneity, institutional break tests.
